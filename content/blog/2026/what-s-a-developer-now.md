I recently watched this [video on YouTube by Primeagen+Mo Bitar](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_vB0PDzaa7I).
Like him or not, this is a video that greatly resonated with me.
A few key takeaways from the video are summarized below.

# "We feel disconnected from the product we are building, making it hard to sell, making it hard to care."

It took me quite a while to get on the AI bandwagon. This is also covered, to some extent, in the video. People who got
on the AI bandwagon early were ... let's call it a hot take, bad developers who suddenly had a newfound ability to
write passable code.

Nowadays, AI is still writing AI slop, but it's not as bad as it used to be. Right now feel a good time to join the party.

Going back to the point now, I do feel disconnected from the product I am building. I always felt attached to the products I built.
It came with pros and cons. 

The pros were that I could sell the product. When I logged out, usually very late at night, I was proud of the work done.
I was waking up with new ideas a drive to see the product through to completion.

For the cons, it also meant I have not taken user feedback as much as I should have. It also meant I might have been more hesitant 
to work on features that I was sure brought no value to the product. Or that I was not the happiest person when I was asked to change
 the name of the product for the fourth time.

Now with AI? I couldn't care less about the 10th change of name for the product. AI is just going to take care of that.
You want to start developing a new feature for the product, that really should have taken more time to think about?
You want to put that new feature and do some A/B testing?

Let's do it. The mental cost to ship a new feature is so low now...

But I don't feel attached to the product I am building anymore. I am not the one building the product anymore. "Something" else is doing it.
I don't know how to sell this. I'm not a vendor at heart and to be honest, maybe there's something to learn for me there.

# Technical skill matters, but so do communication, consistency, and resilience.

Consistency has always been a key factor for me in choosing an employee. I much prefer working with someone who is consistent than a genius who can't communicate 
or deliver only once very 2 months. I am not going to over the tale of the turtle and the rabbit at this point. Consistency is a is rare skills to have
in development, but, I feel like AI is going to change this. 

Doing development, at high levels, is really tiring mentally. AI changes that. 
You can totally offset that mental charge to the AI. You can finish your day with a lot more energy than before.
It's much easier to be consistent when you're not constantly being challenged.

# The hardest part of modern engineering isn't the syntax, it's managing the complexity and the human element.

Well yeah. As developers, we often judge ourselves by the quality of code we write. But that's not what clients are looking for.
They are searching for experts that can guide them and reassure them. For us, that's a given, but for them, it's all that matters.

# So what's a developer now?

I think that's an important question to ask ourselves. Are we all prompt engineers now? Are we the authors of the next century?
Will I ever be proud of a new product I build? Will I ever wake up thinking, what awesome prompt is waiting for me today?
