I recently watched this [video on YouTube by Primeagen + Mo Bitar](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_vB0PDzaa7I).

::YouTube{id="_vB0PDzaa7I"}
::

Like them or not, it's a video that resonated deeply with me.
The video is almost an hour, so here's a few key takeaways.

## "It's highly addictive"

Honestly, it's a game-changer. I'm shipping features faster than ever, what used to be a multi-week slog now takes just a couple of days.
I'm finally starting projects that I used to talk myself out of, either because the ROI wasn't there or, let's be real, I was just too lazy to deal with the setup.

Case in point: this blog.
It’s been languishing on Nuxt 2 for way longer than it should have, mostly because I couldn't find the energy to face the migration.

## "You can't review AI code."

I want to say I review the code, but that’s a lie.
You start with good intentions, but as the AI consistently delivers 'good enough' results, your guard drops.
By the time the code reaches a certain scale, you’re no longer a reviewer. You’re just a passenger.

## "We feel disconnected from the product we are building, making it hard to sell, making it hard to care."

It took me quite a while to get on the AI bandwagon. This is also covered, to some extent, in the video. People who got
on the AI bandwagon early were ... let's call it a hot take, bad developers who suddenly had a newfound ability to
write passable code.

Nowadays, AI is still writing "slop," but it's not as bad as it used to be. Right now feels like a good time to join the party.

Going back to the point now, I do feel disconnected from the product I am building. I always felt attached to the products I built.
It came with pros and cons. 

The pros were that I could sell the product. When I logged out, usually very late at night, I was proud of the work done.
I was waking up with new ideas and a drive to see the product through to completion.

For the cons, it also meant I did not take user feedback as much as I should have. It also meant I might have been more hesitant to work on features that I was sure brought no value to the product. Or that I was not the happiest person when I was asked to change the name of the product for the fourth time.

Now with AI? I couldn't care less about the tenth name change. AI is just going to take care of that.
Want to start developing a new feature that really should have taken more time to think about?
Want to push that new feature and do some A/B testing?

Let's do it. The mental cost to ship a new feature is so low now...

But I don't feel attached to the product I am building anymore. I am not the one building the product anymore. "Something" else is doing it.
I don't know how to sell this. I'm not a salesperson at heart, and to be honest, maybe there's something for me to learn there.

## Technical skill matters, but so do communication, consistency, and resilience.

Consistency has always been a key factor for me in choosing an employee. I much prefer working with someone who is consistent than a genius who can't communicate 
or delivers only once every 2 months. I am not going to go over the tale of the tortoise and the hare at this point. Consistency is a rare skill to have
in development, but I feel like AI is going to change this. 

Developing at high levels is mentally exhausting. AI changes that. 
You can totally offset that mental load to the AI. You can finish your day with a lot more energy than before.
It's much easier to be consistent when you're not constantly being challenged.

## The hardest part of modern engineering isn't the syntax, it's managing the complexity and the human element.

Well, yeah. As developers, we often judge ourselves by the quality of the code we write. But that's not what clients are looking for.
They are searching for experts who can guide and reassure them. For us, that's a given; but for them, it's all that matters.

## So what's a developer now?

I think that's an important question to ask ourselves. Are we all prompt engineers now? Are we the authors of the next century?
Will I ever be proud of a new product I build? Will I ever wake up thinking, "What awesome prompt is waiting for me today?"

Maybe I should really focus on "how can I help this new client" instead of "what awesome product I can build today".
